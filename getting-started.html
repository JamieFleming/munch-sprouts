Getting Started with BLW

Everything you need to know to begin your baby led weaning journey with confidence.

6M+ Recommended age

Is My Baby Ready?

Most babies are ready around 6 months — look for all three signs:

Sits Upright Independently

Can sit in a high chair without slumping, with good head control and able to turn their head side to side.

Lost the Tongue-Thrust Reflex

No longer automatically pushes food out of their mouth with their tongue when it touches their lips.

Shows Interest in Food

Watches others eat with curiosity, reaches out for food, and opens their mouth when they see food approaching.

Good Head Control

Can hold their head steadily and move it to show interest or turn away from food — an important safety signal.

Important: Always consult your GP or Health Visitor before starting weaning. Every baby is different — don't start before 17 weeks (4 months).

Core Principles of BLW

The foundation of a successful weaning journey.

1

Baby is in Control

Let your baby decide what, how much, and how fast they eat. Never force or pressure them — they'll eat when hungry and stop when full.

Self-Regulation
2

Food Before One is Just for Fun

Breast milk or formula remains the primary nutrition source in the first year. Solid foods are for exploration, practice, and fun.

Low Pressure
3

Include Baby in Family Meals

Whenever possible, let baby join family mealtimes. They learn by watching and enjoy the social experience of eating together.

Social Learning
4

Offer a Variety of Foods

Expose your baby to different flavours, textures, and colours from early on to help develop a well-rounded palate and reduce fussy eating later.

Variety

Feeding Techniques & Methods

How to prepare and serve food safely for baby led weaning.

Food Size & Shape

Cut foods into pieces roughly the size of an adult finger — long enough for baby to grip with their whole fist with some sticking out the top.

  • • Sticks about 2 inches long and ½ inch wide
  • • Offer smaller pieces as pincer grasp develops (~8–10 months)
  • • Avoid round or hard shapes that could block the airway

Texture Guidelines

Foods should be soft enough to squish easily between your finger and thumb — but not so mushy they fall apart when picked up.

  • • Ripe avocado, banana, or well-cooked sweet potato
  • • Steam harder vegetables until fork-tender
  • • Meat should be moist and tender — slow-cooked or shredded

Meal Structure

Offer 2–3 foods at each meal from different food groups:

  • ✓ A protein (meat, fish, eggs, beans, tofu)
  • ✓ A fruit or vegetable
  • ✓ A carbohydrate (bread, pasta, rice, potato)

Important Safety Note

Always supervise your baby during every meal. Never leave them alone while eating, even for a moment.

Foods to Avoid in the First Year

Never give these to babies under 12 months

Choking Hazards

✕

Whole Nuts & Peanuts

Crush or flake for under 5s

✕

Whole Grapes & Cherry Tomatoes

Always cut lengthways into quarters

✕

Popcorn, Marshmallows & Jelly Cubes

Choking hazard

✕

Hard or Sticky Sweets

Boiled sweets, chewing gum, cough sweets

✕

Raw Hard Vegetables

Raw carrot, apple, celery — always cook until soft

✕

Peanut Butter by the Spoon

Spread thinly on toast instead

Other Foods to Avoid

✕

Honey

Risk of infant botulism — avoid until 12 months

✕

Added Salt & Salty Foods

Bacon, sausages, crisps, stock cubes — harmful to kidneys

✕

Added Sugar & Sugary Snacks

Causes tooth decay — not needed in baby's diet

✕

Soft Cheeses

Brie, camembert, blue cheese — risk of listeria

✕

Raw Shellfish & Fresh Pâté

Risk of food poisoning — always cook shellfish thoroughly

✕

Shark, Swordfish & Marlin

High mercury levels — can affect baby's nervous system

Drinks to Avoid

Stick to breast milk, formula, and water with meals

✕

Fruit Juice & Smoothies

High in sugar — avoid before 12 months

✕

Squash, Fizzy & Flavoured Drinks

High sugar — causes tooth decay

✕

Cow's Milk as Main Drink

Not before 12 months — OK in small amounts in cooking

✕

Rice Drinks

Contain arsenic — avoid until at least 5 years old

✕

Tea, Coffee & Caffeine

Not suitable for babies or young children

✕

Plant-Based Drinks (Soya, Oat, Almond)

Not before 12 months — OK as part of balanced diet after

Introducing Allergens

Current research suggests introducing the 14 common allergens early (around 6 months) and regularly. Introduce one at a time in small amounts and watch for any reaction for 2–3 days before introducing another.

Peanuts Dairy Soy Fish Eggs Tree Nuts Wheat Shellfish Sesame Molluscs Mustard Celery Sulphites Lupin

Signs of a Food Allergy

After introducing a new allergen, watch for 1 or more of these reactions:

•

Diarrhoea or vomiting

•

A cough

•

Wheezing and shortness of breath

•

Itchy throat and tongue

•

Itchy skin or rash

•

Swollen lips and throat

•

Runny or blocked nose

•

Sore, red and itchy eyes

Severe Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

In a few cases, foods can cause a severe allergic reaction that can be life-threatening. Call 999 and get medical help immediately.

For more information, see food allergies in babies and young children on the NHS website.

Setting Up for Success

Essential equipment and environment tips for happy BLW mealtimes.

Essential Equipment

Sturdy High Chair

Allows your baby to sit upright with feet supported. Proper posture is essential for safe eating.

Long-Sleeved Bibs

Catch mess and protect clothes. Silicone bibs with a pocket are great for collecting dropped food!

Splat Mat

Place under the high chair for quick and easy cleanup. A shower curtain works just as well!

Open Cup for Water

Start practising with a small open cup or free-flow sippy cup. Water with meals is recommended from 6 months.

Creating the Right Environment

Choose the Right Time

Start when your baby is happy and alert — not overtired or very hungry. Mid-morning or early afternoon often works best.

Eat Together

Sit with your baby and eat your meal too. They learn by watching and love the social experience of shared mealtimes.

Stay Calm About Mess

Mess is learning! Expect food everywhere. This exploration is how babies discover textures, temperatures, and tastes.

Pro Tips

  • ✓Strip baby to their nappy in warm weather for easy cleanup
  • ✓Keep warm wipes handy for quick face and hand cleans
  • ✓Take photos and videos — these moments are precious!

Preparing Food for Your Baby

Good food preparation habits keep your baby safe and help them get the most from every meal.

Clean Hands & Surfaces

Always wash your hands thoroughly before preparing food. Keep surfaces, boards, and utensils clean and separate from raw meat prep areas.

Wash All Fruit & Veg

Rinse fruit and vegetables thoroughly under cold running water before peeling, cutting, or cooking — even if they'll be peeled.

Cook Food Thoroughly

Meat, fish, and eggs should be cooked all the way through. Vegetables should be steamed or roasted until soft enough to squish between your fingers.

Cut to the Right Size

Cut food into finger-length strips your baby can hold in their fist with some sticking out. As their pincer grasp develops (~9 months), smaller pieces become suitable.

No Added Salt, Sugar, or Honey

Don't add salt or sugar when cooking for your baby — their kidneys can't handle salt, and sugar encourages a sweet tooth. Honey must be avoided entirely until 12 months.

Check the Temperature

Always test food temperature before serving. Food should be lukewarm — never hot. Test by touching a piece to the inside of your wrist before placing it in front of your baby.

For full NHS guidance on preparing food safely for babies, visit NHS: Preparing food safely →

BLW Timeline & Progression

What to expect at each stage as your baby grows.

6M

Starting Out

Begin with simple, single-ingredient foods. Baby uses a palmar grasp (whole hand). Most food ends up on the floor — completely normal! Focus on exploration, not nutrition.

1–2 meals per day
7–8M

Building Skills

More food makes it to the mouth! Start introducing more variety and combination meals. Begin offering water in a small open cup at mealtimes.

2–3 meals per day
9–11M

Pincer Grasp

Pincer grasp develops (thumb and index finger). Baby can handle smaller pieces and eating becomes more efficient. You'll notice them eating noticeably more.

3 meals + 1–2 snacks
12M+

Family Foods

Baby integrates fully into family meals and can handle most textures. Solid foods become the primary nutrition source. Preferences and dislikes start to show — stay patient and keep offering variety!

3 meals + 2 snacks
