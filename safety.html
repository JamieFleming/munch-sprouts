Baby Safety Guide

Essential safety knowledge for confident, worry-free weaning. Know what to do before you need to.

LOUD Gagging = normal
QUIET Choking = emergency
999 Always supervise

Know What to Do in an Emergency

Before starting BLW, it is strongly recommended to take an infant CPR and choking course. Know the difference between gagging (normal and protective) and choking (rare but serious). If your baby cannot breathe, cough, or cry — call 999 immediately.

Choking & Gagging

Knowing the difference is one of the most important things you can learn before starting BLW.

LOUD

Gagging — baby is making sounds. Normal and protective while they learn to eat.

QUIET

Choking — silent or unable to make sounds. Act immediately.

Gagging (Normal)

Signs:

  • Gagging is loud — baby is making sounds
  • Face may turn red (harder to see on brown or black skin)
  • Eyes may water
  • Tongue may push forward or out of their mouth
  • May make a retching movement, or vomit

What to Do:

  • Stay calm — gagging is normal and protective
  • Do not put your fingers in their mouth
  • Let your baby work through it themselves
  • Offer calm reassurance with your voice
  • Continue the meal once resolved

Choking (Emergency)

Signs:

  • Choking is quiet — silent or unable to make sounds
  • Cannot cough, cry, or breathe properly
  • On lighter skin: lips or face may begin to look blue (cyanosis)
  • On brown or black skin: check gums, inside of lips, or fingernails for a blue tinge
  • May lose consciousness

What to Do:

  • Shout for help immediately
  • Get baby out of the high chair
  • Support their chest and chin with one hand
  • Using the heel of your hand, give 5 sharp blows between the shoulder blades — check after each blow
  • If unsuccessful, call 999 and continue first aid
  • If required, give up to 5 chest thrusts

IMPORTANT: This is not a substitute for proper first aid training. Always seek professional medical help in an emergency.

It is strongly recommended to complete an infant CPR and choking course before starting BLW.

Food Risks & Safe Preparation

Understanding which foods need extra care — and how to prepare them safely.

What Makes a Food Safe for BLW?

Soft & Squishable

Can be squished flat between your thumb and finger with little effort. If you can't squish it easily, your baby can't either.

Finger-Sized Strips

Long enough to grip with their whole fist with some sticking out the top. Roughly the length and width of an adult finger.

Dissolves Easily

Breaks down with saliva or gentle gum pressure — no teeth needed. Soft-cooked veg, banana, and well-cooked pasta are ideal.

Not Round or Hard

Avoid round shapes and hard raw foods. Whole grapes, cherry tomatoes, and raw carrot are classic hazards that fit perfectly in a baby's airway.

High-Risk Foods & How to Make Them Safer

✕

Grapes & Cherry Tomatoes

Risk: Round shapes can block the airway

Make Safe: Always cut lengthways, then into quarters for babies under 12 months

✕

Whole Nuts & Peanuts

Risk: Can get lodged in the throat

Make Safe: Crush or flake for under 5s; spread nut butter thinly on toast

✕

Raw Hard Vegetables

Risk: Too hard to chew safely

Make Safe: Steam or roast carrot, apple, and celery until fork-tender; grate finely as an alternative

✕

Hot Dogs & Sausages

Risk: Round, slippery shape

Make Safe: Cut lengthways into thin strips, then into small bite-sized pieces

✕

Blueberries & Small Round Fruits

Risk: Small and round

Make Safe: Squish flat or cut in half for babies under 9 months

✕

Bread & Nut Butters

Risk: Can form sticky balls in the mouth

Make Safe: Lightly toast bread; spread nut butter thinly to add moisture

Always Avoid (Under 12 Months)

These should not be given to babies under 12 months

✕

Honey

Risk of infant botulism — avoid in cooking too

✕

Added Salt & Salty Foods

Bacon, sausages, crisps, stock cubes — harmful to kidneys

✕

Soft Cheeses

Brie, camembert, blue cheese — risk of listeria

✕

Shark, Swordfish & Marlin

High mercury levels — can affect baby's nervous system

✕

Raw Shellfish & Fresh Pâté

Risk of food poisoning

✕

Popcorn, Jelly Cubes & Marshmallows

Choking hazard — hard to control in the mouth

Food Allergies & Allergens

Current guidance recommends introducing all 14 common allergens early — from around 6 months — one at a time.

1

Introduce One at a Time

Introduce each allergen separately in a small amount. Wait 2–3 days before introducing the next to monitor for any reaction.

2

Introduce Regularly

Once an allergen has been safely introduced, continue to include it regularly in your baby's diet. Regular exposure helps prevent sensitisation.

3

Introduce Early

Current research (including the LEAP study) suggests early introduction from around 6 months helps reduce the risk of developing a food allergy.

The 14 Common Allergens

Peanuts Dairy Soy Fish Eggs Tree Nuts Wheat Shellfish Sesame Molluscs Mustard Celery Sulphites Lupin

Signs of a Food Allergy

After introducing a new allergen, watch for 1 or more of these reactions:

•

Diarrhoea or vomiting

•

A cough

•

Wheezing and shortness of breath

•

Itchy throat and tongue

•

Itchy skin or rash

•

Swollen lips and throat

•

Runny or blocked nose

•

Sore, red and itchy eyes

Severe Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

In rare cases, foods can trigger anaphylaxis — a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction. Call 999 immediately if you suspect anaphylaxis. Signs include sudden swelling of the lips, tongue or throat, difficulty breathing, or collapse.

For more information, see food allergies in babies and young children on the NHS website.

If you suspect your baby has a food allergy, stop giving them that food and speak to your GP or health visitor as soon as possible. Do not attempt to reintroduce it without medical guidance.

Safe Mealtimes

Safe habits at every meal — from how baby sits to how you store leftovers.

Safe Eating Environment

Always Supervise

  • Stay within arm's reach at all times
  • Give your baby your full attention
  • No eating in the car or pushchair
  • Avoid distractions (TV, mobile phone)

Proper Positioning

  • Baby sits fully upright in a high chair
  • Feet supported on a footrest
  • Never reclined or slouched
  • Secured with high chair straps

Let Baby Set the Pace

  • Never rush meals or force feed
  • Allow time to chew and swallow fully
  • Watch for signs of fullness
  • Let baby decide how much to eat

Check Food Temperature

  • Always test temperature before serving
  • Food should be lukewarm, not hot
  • Stir thoroughly to avoid hot spots
  • Test on the inside of your wrist

Calm Environment

  • Turn off screens and reduce noise
  • Focus on eating, not playing
  • Remove toys from the tray
  • Eat together to model good habits

Baby's State

  • Fully alert and awake before eating
  • Not overtired or very cranky
  • Not excessively hungry — a little hungry is ideal
  • In a happy, calm mood

Storing & Reheating Food

Cooling & Refrigerating

  • Cool hot food within 1–2 hours
  • Store in a covered container in the fridge
  • Use refrigerated food within 24 hours

Freezing

  • Freeze portions for up to 3 months
  • Use ice cube trays and label with the date
  • Defrost in the fridge overnight — never on the worktop

Reheating

  • Reheat once only — never reheat the same food twice
  • Heat until piping hot all the way through, then cool before serving
  • Stir well to distribute heat evenly and avoid hot spots

Leftovers & Serving

  • Always decant from jars or pouches into a bowl before serving
  • Discard any leftover food after a meal — saliva from the spoon contaminates it
  • Don't refreeze food that has already been defrosted

When in doubt, throw it out. It is always safer to prepare a fresh portion than risk food that may have spoiled.

Emergency Preparedness Checklist

Before you start BLW, make sure you have:

  • Emergency numbers saved — especially 999
  • Completed an infant CPR and first aid course
  • Discussed BLW with your GP or health visitor
  • Practised the infant choking response technique
  • Read up on allergen introduction guidance
